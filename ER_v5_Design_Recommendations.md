## 3. Answering Research Questions

### 3.1 Why blank paper?
- **ER conditions change rapidly** – patients deteriorate, new information arrives, priorities shift.
- **Structured sheets can become obsolete** within minutes.
- **Flexibility** allows nurses to adapt documentation to the actual situation.
- **Recommendation:** Provide **structured flexibility** – dedicated sections with ample blank space for improvisation, a “rapid changes” area, and modular design that can be ignored when not needed.

### 3.2 What sells on Etsy?
- **Structured but flexible templates** – enough guidance to be useful, not so rigid they hinder workflow.
- **4‑8 patient sheets** – balance between detail and coverage.
- **Printable PDFs** – easy to print at home or work, no special software.
- **Bundles** – multiple versions (different patient counts, orientations, specialties).
- **Recommendation:** Offer a **bundle of printable PDFs** with 4, 6, and 8‑patient versions; include both portrait and landscape options.

### 3.3 Optimal patient count?
- **Research suggests 4 patients on 1 page** balances detail vs coverage.
- **Etsy top sellers range 4‑8**, indicating market acceptance of higher densities for less‑acuity settings.
- **Recommendation:** Design **two primary variants**:
  - **4‑patient detailed sheet** – more space per patient, ideal for high‑acuity/trauma.
  - **8‑patient summary sheet** – compact, for fast‑track/low‑acuity patients.
  - Allow nurses to choose based on shift acuity.

### 3.4 Layout preference?
- **Likely portrait** – standard paper orientation, easier to handle, fits clipboards, familiar.
- **Landscape** offers wider columns for vitals grids and timelines, but may be less convenient.
- **Recommendation:** Default to **portrait for general ER**, offer **landscape for trauma/high‑acuity** where wider grids are beneficial. Provide both in the bundle.

### 3.5 Critical fields?
- Based on Etsy descriptions and clinical practice:
  - **Patient info** (name, age, MRN, room)
  - **Vitals** (BP, HR, RR, SpO₂, temp, pain)
  - **Meds** (timestamps, dose, route)
  - **Tasks** (to‑do list, pending orders)
  - **Notes** (free‑text for trends, changes)
- **Additional ER‑specific fields:**
  - **ESI level** (acuity color‑coding)
  - **Allergies**
  - **Code status**
  - **Isolation precautions**
  - **Fall risk**
  - **IV access** (site, gauge)
  - **Labs/imaging ordered**
  - **Procedures performed**
  - **Handoff** (SBAR or similar)
- **Recommendation:** Prioritize fields by frequency of use. Use **modular sections** that can be skipped if irrelevant.

## 4. ER v5 Design Recommendations

### 4.1 Core Principles
1. **Structured Flexibility** – provide guidance but leave room for improvisation.
2. **Market‑Proven Configurations** – 4‑8 patients per page, printable PDFs, bundles.
3. **New‑Grad Friendly** – clear labels, examples, glossary of terms.
4. **Shift‑Handoff Optimized** – dedicated SBAR section with prompts.
5. **Efficiency‑Focused** – checkboxes, pre‑printed common meds/procedures, minimal writing.

### 4.2 Recommended Specifications
| Aspect | Recommendation | Rationale |
|--------|---------------|-----------|
| **Primary Orientation** | Portrait | Standard paper, easier handling, fits clipboards |
| **Patients per Page** | 4 (detailed) & 8 (summary) | Balance detail vs coverage, market validation |
| **Page Size** | US Letter (8.5″ × 11″) | Universal printing compatibility |
| **Color Coding** | ESI‑based highlight tints | Visual acuity recognition, optional B&W version |
| **Sections per Patient** | 1. Quick Identifiers<br>2. Vitals Grid (time‑based)<br>3. Meds/Tasks<br>4. Notes/Rapid Changes<br>5. Handoff (SBAR) | Logical flow, prioritized by urgency |
| **Flexibility Feature** | “Rapid Changes” blank area | Allows improvisation for evolving conditions |
| **Bundle Contents** | 4‑patient portrait, 8‑patient portrait, 4‑patient landscape, 8‑patient landscape | Cater to different acuity levels and preferences |

### 4.3 Section Details
**Quick Identifiers:**
- Name, Age, MRN, Room
- ESI (color‑coded checkbox)
- Allergies, Code Status, Isolation, Fall Risk
- Chief Complaint, Arrival Time

**Vitals Grid:**
- Time, BP, HR, RR, SpO₂, Temp, Pain (0‑10), Neuro Checks
- 8‑12 rows with write‑in times

**Meds/Tasks:**
- Pre‑printed common ED meds (checkboxes)
- Timestamped rows for additional meds
- To‑do checklist (labs, imaging, consults, discharge)

**Notes/Rapid Changes:**
- 3‑4 blank lines for free‑text notes
- Labeled “Rapid Changes / Unexpected Events”

**Handoff (SBAR):**
- Prompted lines for Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation
- Space for next nurse’s signature/initials

### 4.4 Visual Design
- **Clean, sans‑serif fonts** (Arial, Helvetica)
- **Light borders** between sections
- **Alternating row shading** in grids
- **Color‑coded ESI highlights** (subtle tints, B&W safe)
- **Ample white space** to reduce visual clutter

### 4.5 Implementation Steps
1. **Create high‑fidelity mock‑ups** for 4‑patient portrait and 8‑patient portrait.
2. **Test with ER nurses** (virtual or in‑person) for usability feedback.
3. **Iterate** based on feedback, especially on flexibility and new‑grad clarity.
4. **Produce printable PDFs** with fillable fields (optional).
5. **Package as a bundle** on Etsy or similar platform.
6. **Develop a digital version** for tablet use (future phase).

## 5. Risk Mitigation

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|------------|
| **Too rigid** – nurses reject structured sheets | Keep ample blank space, design modular sections that can be ignored |
| **Too complex** – overwhelms new grads | Provide examples, glossary, simplified version |
| **Printing issues** – margins, colors | Test on common printers, offer B&W version |
| **Low adoption** – nurses prefer blank paper | Emphasize time‑saving benefits, include “flexibility” as a key selling point |
